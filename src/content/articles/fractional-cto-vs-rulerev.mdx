South African founders ask me this directly, often after the discovery call has already established that we get on. "Do I need a technical co-founder, or is what you do at RuleRev enough?" The answer depends on what you are building, what you are raising, and what you are willing to give up. This piece is the comparison written without the agency-marketing softening, so you can decide before the next conversation.

## The three options on the table

There are three real options for a non-technical founder in South Africa who needs serious technical capacity. They are not interchangeable, and the wrong choice is expensive in different ways.

The first is a technical co-founder. Equity partner. Builds the product. Stays for the duration. The second is a fractional CTO. Senior engineer or ex-CTO, retained for one to three days a week, sets direction, hires the team you eventually build under them. The third is a productised studio engagement - what I do at RuleRev. Fixed scope, fixed timeline, named deliverables, shipped end to end.

Each of these solves a different problem. Choosing between them by cost is the wrong test. Choosing by what your product needs at the stage you are in is the right test.

## When a technical co-founder is right

If you are pre-revenue, you are planning to raise institutional capital within 12 months, and the product is technically deep enough that the build never really finishes - a technical co-founder is the right answer.

The reason is not that contractors cannot ship. The reason is that the deepest technical decisions on a venture-backed product are made every week for years. The encryption posture, the data model, the platform choice, the rebuild from monolith to services, the migration to a new cloud, the trade-off between speed and reliability when the next product feature lands - those decisions need somebody who is going to live with the consequences for the lifetime of the company.

A technical co-founder will out-care any contractor. They will out-stay any contractor. They will absorb the bad weeks when the contractor would have moved on to a project that pays. The equity is the price, and at that stage of company it is the right price. Co-founder equity is typically in the 15% to 50% range; for a serious technical co-founder on a venture-track product in South Africa, expect to be at the higher end of that range.

The catch is supply. There are not many technical co-founders looking for a non-technical founder to partner with in South Africa right now. The pool is competitive, slow to convert, and the conversation is months long. If you start the search today and you sign a co-founder in six months, that is a normal timeline. If your product needs to be live in three months for a fundraise, the technical co-founder path does not match the schedule.

## When a fractional CTO is right

If you have early revenue or product-market fit, a small engineering team, and you need somebody senior to set direction without paying a full-time CTO salary - a fractional CTO is the right answer.

The fractional CTO model assumes there is a team to direct. They are not the person writing all the code. They are the person who sets the architecture, runs the hiring loop, owns the on-call rotation, and translates between the founder and the engineers. Three to six months is the typical engagement. By the end of it, the team they have built can run without them, and the engagement either ends or steps down to advisory.

Fractional CTO day rates in South Africa for somebody with real CTO experience sit in the R12 000 to R25 000 per day range. Two days a week is roughly R20 000 to R45 000 a week, or R80 000 to R180 000 a month. That is meaningfully cheaper than a full-time CTO with equity, and it is meaningfully more expensive than a junior team without one. The maths works when there is enough product complexity to need the senior judgment but not enough revenue to justify the full-time hire yet.

The mismatch happens when there is no team yet. A fractional CTO with no engineers to direct is just an expensive consultant writing strategy docs. If you are pre-team and pre-revenue, the fractional CTO model is the wrong shape.

RuleRev is the right call when:

- You have a specific thing that needs to ship, with a specific deadline (often a fundraise, a launch, or a regulatory date).
- You are not yet at the size where you need a full-time technical leader, and you do not yet have the team a fractional CTO would direct.

## The decision matrix in one table

The honest comparison is across four variables: cost, time-to-shipped, skin in the game, and what you keep at the end.

| | Technical co-founder | Fractional CTO | RuleRev |
|---|---|---|---|
| Up-front cost | Low cash, high equity (15–50%) | Moderate (R80k–R180k/month) | Fixed price per engagement |

| Skin in the game | Maximum | Engagement-bounded | Outcome-bounded |

The table is not a recommendation. It is a way to see the question without the marketing.

## How to switch from one to another later

This is the question I get most often, and the answer is reassuring. The three options compose. They do not compete.

Many of the founders I work with hire RuleRev for the Founder Launch Pack first. The site is shipped, the compliance pack is shipped, the architecture is in place, and they go and raise on that base. After the raise, they hire engineers and bring in a fractional CTO to direct the new team. After the team scales past five engineers, the fractional CTO either steps up to full-time or hands over to a permanent CTO hired into the role.

The base that gets shipped at the start does not need to be rebuilt. That is partly because RuleRev's house style is to ship boring, well-understood architecture - Terraform-everything, managed services, no exotic dependencies - and partly because a sensible production starting point is roughly the same regardless of who builds it.

You can also start with a fractional CTO and bring in RuleRev for a specific deliverable that the team does not have the bandwidth or the skills to ship. POPIA retrofits are a common example: the team is full of product engineers, and a one-week compliance retrofit is the shape that does not fit on their backlog. That engagement also composes cleanly.

The composition does not work in one direction. If you bring in a co-founder after RuleRev or a fractional CTO has already shipped a meaningful chunk of the platform, the co-founder equity conversation is harder, because the platform exists and the co-founder is joining a thing that has already moved. Plan around that.
